Optimizing UI/UX for performance

Techniques for designing interfaces that are fast, responsive, and user-friendly.

Published on

February 05, 2025

Author

Jonas Eriksson

Time to read

5 min read

Tags

performanceux

Performance is a feature. A product that loads slowly or feels sluggish has a poor user experience regardless of how good it looks or how thoughtfully it's been designed.

The challenge for design engineers is to create experiences that are both visually rich and technically fast — to refuse the false tradeoff between beauty and performance.

Perceived Performance vs. Actual Performance

Users care about how fast your product feels, not just how fast it technically is. Perceived performance is influenced by many factors beyond raw speed: what content appears first, whether transitions feel smooth, whether interactions respond immediately, whether loading states communicate progress.

Understanding this distinction opens up a rich design space. Sometimes you can dramatically improve perceived performance without changing technical performance at all — simply by being thoughtful about what loads first and how loading states are designed.

Design Patterns for Performance

Progressive enhancement means building the core experience first and layering complexity on top. Skeleton screens reduce the perceived wait time by showing the structure of content before it loads. Optimistic UI makes interactions feel instant by assuming success and only correcting on failure.
