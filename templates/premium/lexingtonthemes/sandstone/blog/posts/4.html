Architecture that supports well-being

Evidence-based design moves that help healthcare and workplace projects nurture calm, connection, and recovery.

The built environment has a measurable impact on human health, mood, and performance. Evidence-based design—the practice of grounding design decisions in research about how physical environments affect people—has moved from the edges of the profession to become a mainstream expectation, particularly in healthcare and workplace contexts.

Biophilic design

Access to nature, whether through views of green space, natural materials, or indoor planting, consistently improves well-being outcomes in studies across workplace and clinical settings. Incorporating biophilic elements—living walls, natural timber, water features, and views of sky and landscape—is one of the most reliable tools for creating spaces that support human flourishing.

Light and circadian health

The timing, intensity, and color temperature of light profoundly affects circadian rhythms, sleep quality, and alertness. Dynamic lighting systems that mimic the shift from cool morning light to warm afternoon and evening tones help occupants maintain healthy biological rhythms, particularly in environments like healthcare facilities where natural light access is often limited.

Acoustic comfort

Noise stress is one of the most commonly cited environmental discomforts in workplaces and healthcare settings. Acoustic planning—through material selection, space layout, and active noise-masking systems—creates environments where people can concentrate, communicate clearly, and recover without sensory overload.
